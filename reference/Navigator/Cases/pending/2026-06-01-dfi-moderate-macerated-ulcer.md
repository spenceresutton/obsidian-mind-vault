# SSTI Case 14: Moderate Diabetic Foot Infection with Macerated Ulcer

**Difficulty:** medium
**Author:** Claude

## Prompt

SS is a 59-year-old female with a past medical history significant for type 2 diabetes mellitus, peripheral neuropathy, and hypertension who presents to the emergency department with a worsening wound on the plantar surface of her left foot. She reports noticing an ulcer approximately 2 weeks ago and has been soaking her foot in warm water once or twice daily for the past 10 days at the recommendation of a family member, hoping to "soften the skin and help it heal." She denies fever but reports increasing tenderness and swelling over the past 4 days.

On arrival, vitals are: T 37.6°C, HR 90, BP 128/78, RR 16, SpO2 98% on room air. The left foot shows a 3 × 2.5 cm ulcer at the plantar surface of the third and fourth metatarsal heads. The surrounding skin and wound edges are macerated and boggy from repeated soaking. Erythema extends 3–4 cm from the wound margin with moderate warmth and tenderness. There is a moderate amount of malodorous, watery discharge. No purulent pus. Probe-to-bone test is negative. No crepitus. Posterior tibial and dorsalis pedis pulses are 2+ bilaterally. She takes metformin, lisinopril, and amlodipine. She has no known drug allergies.

**Ht:** 5'4"   **Wt:** 82 kg   **BMI:** 31.5   **Baseline SCr:** 0.9 mg/dL   **Allergies:** NKDA

## Expected Output

- **Presentation:** Moderate diabetic foot infection with wound maceration from repeated foot soaking — maceration is a recognized Pseudomonas risk factor; empiric anti-pseudomonal coverage is indicated
- **Antibiotic Initiation** *(if applicable):* Piperacillin-tazobactam 3.375g IV q8h over 4 hours extended infusion (SJN standard protocol; patient is hemodynamically stable, not septic shock — standard extended infusion dose). Pip/tazo provides coverage for aerobic gram-positive cocci, gram-negative rods including Pseudomonas, and anaerobes. Vancomycin should be added if MRSA risk factors are identified. **Podiatry consult recommended** (wound debridement, proper wound care, offloading). **Vascular consult recommended** (vascular assessment in diabetic patients with foot infection). Instruct patient to stop foot soaking immediately — maceration worsens wound healing and promotes pathogen growth.
- **Treatment Optimization** *(if applicable):* Obtain deep wound culture (tissue biopsy preferred over surface swab). If Pseudomonas is not confirmed on culture and patient is improving, de-escalate to a narrower-spectrum agent. Duration for moderate DFI: typically 1–3 weeks based on clinical response and culture data.

**INCORRECT:**
- Cefazolin or amoxicillin-clavulanate without anti-pseudomonal coverage — wound maceration from soaking is an established Pseudomonas risk factor in DFI; narrow-spectrum beta-lactams do not cover Pseudomonas
- Continuing foot soaking — maceration impairs wound healing and promotes infection; this must be stopped as part of wound management
- Omitting podiatry consult — offloading and proper wound care are essential components of DFI management; antibiotic therapy alone is insufficient
