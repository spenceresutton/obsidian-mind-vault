# SJN Inpatient SSTI Guidelines

St. Joseph Hospital, Department of Pharmacy — Antimicrobial Stewardship. Inpatient Skin and Soft Tissue Infection (SSTI) Guidelines. Marked *DRAFT — For Review and Approval*.

## Introduction

These guidelines place a high value on reducing avoidable IV catheter-related adverse events, costs, and resources, and on the practical aspects of antibiotic administration. Core operational principles:

- IV therapy is reserved for patients unable to take PO or whose clinical severity precludes oral therapy. Transition to oral when clinically stable and tolerating PO.
- Antimicrobial choice is adjusted based on wound culture and susceptibility when available. Blood cultures should be obtained for moderate-to-severe infections prior to antibiotic initiation.
- These recommendations support, but do not supersede, provider clinical decision-making. Doses reflect normal renal function unless otherwise noted.
- Obtain MRSA/MSSA nasal PCR in patients empirically covered for MRSA to facilitate de-escalation. A negative MRSA nares PCR has a high negative predictive value for MRSA SSTI.

---

## Definitions

| Classification | Definition |
|----------------|-----------|
| Nonpurulent SSTI | Infection without purulent exudate or exudate not collectible; includes erysipelas and cellulitis |
| Purulent SSTI | Infection with purulent exudate or exudate that is collectible; includes furuncles, carbuncles, and abscesses |
| Necrotizing Fasciitis | Deep-seated infection of the subcutaneous tissue and fascia with progressive destruction of the fascial plane; surgical emergency |
| Diabetic Foot Infection (DFI) | Infection occurring below the malleoli in a person with diabetes; may involve skin, soft tissue, bone, and/or joints |
| MRSA / MSSA | Methicillin-resistant / -susceptible *Staphylococcus aureus* |
| I&D | Incision and drainage |

## Nonpurulent SSTI (Erysipelas and Cellulitis)

**Etiology:** Beta-hemolytic streptococci (predominantly *Streptococcus pyogenes*, Groups B, C, and G); MSSA.

### Empiric Treatment

| Severity | Preferred Regimen | Beta-Lactam Allergy |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Mild** | Cephalexin 500 mg PO q6h | Clindamycin 450 mg PO q8h |
| **Moderate** *(systemic signs of infection)* | Cefazolin 2 g IV q8h | Vancomycin IV |
| **Moderate – MRSA Risk** *(purulent drainage, prior MRSA SSTI within 1 year, penetrating trauma, or IV drug use)* | Vancomycin IV | Vancomycin IV |
| **Severe** *(immunocompromised, hemodynamic instability, or rapid spread)* | Vancomycin IV PLUS Piperacillin/tazobactam 4.5 g IV q8h extended infusion PLUS Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h | Vancomycin IV PLUS Aztreonam 2 g IV q8h PLUS Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h |

### Other Considerations

- Duration of 5 days is standard; may be extended if slow clinical improvement. Reassess at 48–72 hours. Failure to respond should prompt evaluation for alternate diagnosis, deeper infection, or antibiotic modification.
- Elevate and compress legs in lower-extremity cellulitis to reduce edema and accelerate resolution.
- Empiric MRSA coverage is not recommended for mild or moderate disease without risk factors.
- ID and surgical consult is recommended for severe or rapidly progressive disease and when necrotizing fasciitis cannot be excluded.
- In stable patients empirically receiving anti-MRSA therapy, MRSA/MSSA PCR may be utilized to support de-escalation.

## Purulent SSTI (Furuncles, Carbuncles, and Abscesses)

**Etiology:** *Staphylococcus aureus* — both MSSA and MRSA. Gram-negative and anaerobic organisms may be present in perirectal or axillary abscesses.

### Empiric Treatment

| Severity | Empiric Treatment |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Mild** | TMP/SMX 5–10 mg/kg/day PO q8–12h **OR** Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID |
| **Moderate-to-severe** | Vancomycin IV |

### Other Considerations

- Incision and drainage (I&D) is the **primary** treatment for purulent SSTI; antibiotics serve as adjuncts.
- For uncomplicated abscesses in immunocompetent patients, antibiotics after I&D may not be necessary if the infection is mild and adequate drainage is achieved.
- Treatment should be continued for 5 days after I&D if adjunctive antibiotics are used.
- Aspirate or wound culture should be sent to allow culture-guided therapy once susceptibilities are available.
- When culture data is unavailable, MRSA/MSSA PCR may be utilized to support de-escalation in stable patients responding to therapy.

## Necrotizing Fasciitis

### Etiology

- **Type I — Polymicrobial:** Mixed gram-positive, gram-negative, and anaerobic organisms (including Enterobacterales, *Bacteroides* spp., *Peptostreptococcus* spp.); common in trunk, perineum (Fournier's gangrene), and post-surgical sites.
- **Type II — Monomicrobial:** Predominantly Group A *Streptococcus* (*Streptococcus pyogenes*); also MRSA, *Clostridium* spp.; typically occurs in extremities.
- **Type III and IV** represent less common presentations associated with specific pathogens (*Vibrio* spp. and *Candida* spp.) and are outside the scope of this document. Infectious Diseases consultation is advised.

### Clinical Presentation

- Rapidly advancing soft tissue infection with systemic toxicity (fever, tachycardia, hypotension, altered mentation)
- Hard or wooden texture of the skin on palpation; pain disproportionate to appearance is a hallmark
- Induration, bullae, crepitus, skin necrosis, and gray dishwater drainage are late findings

> ⚠ **EMERGENT SURGICAL AND INFECTIOUS DISEASES CONSULT IS REQUIRED FOR ALL SUSPECTED CASES OF NECROTIZING FASCIITIS. DO NOT DELAY SURGICAL DEBRIDEMENT FOR IMAGING IF DIAGNOSIS IS CLINICALLY EVIDENT.**

### Empiric Treatment

| Preferred Regimen | Beta-Lactam Allergy |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Vancomycin IV PLUS Piperacillin/tazobactam 4.5 g IV q8h extended infusion PLUS Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h | Vancomycin IV PLUS Aztreonam 2 g IV q8h PLUS Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h |

### Duration of Therapy

- Continue empiric therapy until the patient is clinically stable, all surgical debridement is complete, and the patient has been afebrile for 48–72 hours.
- Narrow based on operative wound cultures and susceptibilities. Typical total duration is 2–4 weeks depending on extent of infection and clinical response.
- In stable patients without osteomyelitis, bacteremia, or otherwise disseminated infection, antibiotics may be stopped 5 days after last debridement.

### Toxin Suppression

- Linezolid and clindamycin reduce toxin production in necrotizing fasciitis, potentially reducing severity of infection.
- The benefits of toxin production suppression have been demonstrated for monomicrobial infections caused by *Streptococcus pyogenes* and *Clostridium* spp. Some benefit may be seen in infections caused by *Staphylococcus aureus*.
- Continue clindamycin for 3–5 days, through surgical debridement, and until culture results return. Data do not support continuation for confirmed polymicrobial infection once gram-negatives and anaerobes are identified as the primary pathogens.

## Diabetic Foot Infection (DFI)

**Etiology:** Mild infections typically involve skin flora: *Staphylococcus aureus* (including MRSA) and beta-hemolytic streptococci. Moderate-to-severe and chronic infections may involve polymicrobial flora including gram-negative bacilli (*Pseudomonas aeruginosa* in macerated/aquatic-exposed wounds) and anaerobes (ischemic or necrotic wounds).

### Severity Classification (IDSA/IWGDF)

- **Mild:** Superficial infection with **two** of the following — local swelling/induration, local tenderness or pain, local increased warmth, purulent discharge, erythema >0.5 but <2 cm around the wound.
- **Moderate:** Infection with no systemic manifestations but involving — erythema extending ≥2 cm from the wound margin, and/or tissue deeper than skin or subcutaneous tissues (e.g., tendon, muscle, joint, bone).
- **Severe:** Any foot infection associated with systemic manifestation as defined by SIRS criteria.
- Moderate or severe infections involving bone are staged 3-O or 4-O, respectively.

### Empiric Treatment

| IDSA/IWGDF Severity | Preferred Regimen | Beta-Lactam Allergy | Duration |
|---------------------|-------------------|---------------------|----------|
| **Mild — no complicating features** | Cephalexin 500 mg PO q6h | Clindamycin 450 mg PO q8h | 1–2 weeks* |
| **Mild — recent antimicrobial exposure** | Amoxicillin/clavulanate 875-125 mg PO q12h | Levofloxacin 750 mg q24h | 1–2 weeks* |
| **Mild — MRSA risk factors** | TMP/SMX 5–10 mg/kg/day PO q8–12h **OR** Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID | (same) | 1–2 weeks* |
| **Mild — recent antimicrobial exposure AND MRSA risk** | Doxycycline 100 mg BID PLUS Amoxicillin/clavulanate 875-125 mg PO q12h | Doxycycline 100 mg BID PLUS Levofloxacin 750 mg PO q24h | 1–2 weeks* |
| **Moderate-to-severe — no complicating features** | Ampicillin/sulbactam 3 g IV q6h | Vancomycin IV PLUS Aztreonam 2 g IV q8h | 2–4 weeks* |
| **Moderate-to-severe — recent antimicrobial exposure** | Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q24h | Vancomycin IV PLUS Aztreonam 2 g IV q8h | 2–4 weeks* |
| **Moderate-to-severe — recent antimicrobial exposure AND MRSA risk** | Vancomycin IV PLUS Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q24h | Vancomycin IV PLUS Aztreonam 2 g IV q8h | 2–4 weeks* |
| **Moderate-to-severe — macerated ulcer or warm climate** | Piperacillin/tazobactam 4.5 g q8h extended infusion | Vancomycin IV PLUS Aztreonam 2 g IV q8h | 2–4 weeks* |
| **Moderate-to-severe — risk factors for resistant gram-negative organisms** | Meropenem 1 g IV q8h | Meropenem 1 g IV q8h | 2–4 weeks* |

### Other Considerations

- Clinically uninfected wounds should not be treated.
- Ensure anaerobic coverage is included in therapy for diseases associated with ischemic limb, necrosis, or gas formation.
- Curettage or biopsy is recommended to identify the causative microorganism. Surface swabs are unreliable and reflect colonizing flora.
- Evaluate for osteomyelitis in all DFI patients with deep wounds, exposed bone, or infection overlying a bony prominence. Initial assessment should include probe-to-bone, X-ray, and serum biomarkers, with MRI available when diagnosis remains in doubt.
- **MRSA risk factors for DFI:** prolonged hospitalization, intensive care admission, recent hospitalization and antimicrobial use, open wounds, long-term venous access, admission to nursing homes, invasive procedures, and HIV infection.
- **Pseudomonas coverage is not routinely recommended in North America.** Reserve for prior *Pseudomonas* isolation, macerated wounds, or moderate-to-severe disease originating in Asia, North Africa, and other warm climates.
- Vascular Surgery consult is recommended for patients with peripheral arterial disease, as revascularization may be required for wound healing.
- Surgical/Podiatry and Infectious Diseases consultation is strongly recommended for moderate-to-severe DFI and all suspected osteomyelitis cases.
- Oral step-down is appropriate in stable patients without osteomyelitis.

#### Osteomyelitis duration (\*)

| Scenario | Duration |
|----------|----------|
| After resection | 2–5 days |
| After soft tissue debridement | 1–2 weeks |
| After minor amputation with positive margins | 3 weeks |
| Without surgery | 6 weeks |

Standard DFI duration is 1–2 weeks; may extend to 3–4 weeks when infection is extensive and resolution is slow.

### Restricted Antimicrobials

- **Linezolid:** requires Infectious Diseases approval.
- **Meropenem:** requires Infectious Diseases approval.
