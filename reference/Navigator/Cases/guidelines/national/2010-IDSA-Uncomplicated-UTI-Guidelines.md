# 2010 IDSA/ESCMID Uncomplicated UTI Guidelines

## Overview

Focused on treatment of premenopausal, non-pregnant women with no known urological abnormalities or comorbidities presenting with acute uncomplicated cystitis or pyelonephritis. Two clinical questions: optimal cystitis therapy and optimal pyelonephritis therapy.

**Two key principles driving agent selection:**
1. **Resistance** — local in vitro susceptibility patterns must inform empiric choice. TMP/SMX threshold: avoid empiric use if local resistance >20%. FQ threshold for pyelonephritis: if resistance >10%, add initial parenteral agent.
2. **Collateral damage** — ecological adverse effects of antimicrobial therapy (selection of resistant organisms, MRSA, C. difficile). FQs and broad-spectrum cephalosporins carry high collateral damage risk. Nitrofurantoin, fosfomycin, and mecillinam carry minimal collateral damage — preferred for cystitis precisely because of this.

---

## Q1: Acute Uncomplicated Cystitis — Treatment Recommendations

### First-Line Agents (preferred order reflects both efficacy and collateral damage)

| Agent | Dose | Duration | Grade | Notes |
|-------|------|----------|-------|-------|
| Nitrofurantoin monohydrate/macrocrystals | 100mg PO BID | 5 days | A-I | Avoid if early pyelonephritis suspected; CI if CrCl <30 |
| TMP/SMX | 160/800mg (1 DS) PO BID | 3 days | A-I | Avoid if local resistance >20% or used for UTI in prior 3 months |
| Fosfomycin trometamol | 3g PO single dose | 1 dose | A-I | Lower efficacy than nitrofurantoin/TMP-SMX; avoid if early pyelonephritis suspected |
| Pivmecillinam | 400mg PO BID | 3-7 days | A-I | Not available in North America; lower efficacy |

### Alternative Agents (when first-line agents cannot be used)

| Agent | Dose | Duration | Grade | Notes |
|-------|------|----------|-------|-------|
| Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, ofloxacin) | Varies | 3 days | A-I (efficacy) / A-III (recommendation) | NOT first-line — collateral damage (FQ resistance, MRSA); reserve when preferred agents unavailable |
| Beta-lactams (amox-clav, cefdinir, cefaclor, cefpodoxime) | Varies | 3-5 days | B-I | Generally inferior efficacy and more adverse effects; use only when other options not feasible |
| Cephalexin | Less studied | 3-5 days | B-III | May be appropriate in some settings |

### Agents to Avoid

| Agent | Recommendation |
|-------|---------------|
| Amoxicillin or ampicillin | Do NOT use for empirical treatment — poor efficacy, very high resistance rates worldwide (A-III) |

**Critical collateral damage hierarchy:**
- Nitrofurantoin / fosfomycin / mecillinam: minimal → preferred
- TMP/SMX: some effect on intestinal flora, not considered classic "collateral damage"
- Fluoroquinolones: high — MRSA, FQ resistance in gram-negatives including Pseudo
- Broad-spectrum cephalosporins: high — ESBL selection in gram-negatives

**When early pyelonephritis cannot be excluded** (subjective fever, symptoms >5-7 days, vague flank pain): avoid nitrofurantoin and fosfomycin — they don't achieve adequate renal tissue levels.

## Q2: Acute Pyelonephritis — Treatment Recommendations

### Universal Rule
Urine culture and susceptibility ALWAYS required for pyelonephritis (A-III). Tailor therapy when results available.

### Outpatient Pyelonephritis

| Scenario | Agent | Dose | Duration | Grade |
|----------|-------|------|----------|-------|
| FQ resistance <10% | Ciprofloxacin | 500mg PO BID | 7 days | A-I |
| | OR Ciprofloxacin XR | 1000mg PO daily | 7 days | B-II |
| | OR Levofloxacin | 750mg PO daily | 5 days | B-II |
| FQ resistance >10% | Add initial 1-time IV: ceftriaxone 1g OR aminoglycoside (consolidated 24h dose) | — | — | B-III |
| Susceptible organism | TMP/SMX | 160/800mg PO BID | 14 days | A-I |
| Unknown susceptibility + TMP/SMX | Add initial IV ceftriaxone 1g | — | — | B-II |
| Oral beta-lactam | Add initial IV ceftriaxone 1g | — | — | B-III; 10-14 days total |

**TMP/SMX for pyelonephritis:** Only if organism is known susceptible. High rates of treatment failure with resistant organisms. The 14-day duration is FDA-approved; shorter course may be effective but not yet validated by RCT.

**Oral beta-lactams:** Less effective for pyelonephritis — inferior efficacy and higher relapse rates vs. FQs. Use with caution; always bridge with initial parenteral dose.

### Inpatient Pyelonephritis (Hospitalized)

Start IV, choose based on local resistance data, tailor to culture results:
- IV fluoroquinolone, OR
- Aminoglycoside ± ampicillin, OR
- Extended-spectrum cephalosporin or extended-spectrum penicillin ± aminoglycoside, OR
- Carbapenem

Grade: B-III (no adequate RCT data for inpatient; recommendation unchanged from 1999 guideline).

**Ampicillin note:** Given high gram-negative resistance, limit ampicillin to cases with suspected Enterococcus (based on prior history); pair with aminoglycoside.

## Resistance Thresholds — Critical for Empiric Selection

| Drug | Cystitis threshold | Pyelonephritis threshold |
|------|--------------------|--------------------------|
| TMP/SMX | Avoid empirically if resistance >20% | Avoid empirically; must know susceptibility or bridge with parenteral |
| Fluoroquinolones | Reserve for when first-line agents can't be used (resistance trend concern) | Preferred if resistance <10%; add parenteral agent if >10% |
| Nitrofurantoin | Low resistance historically; preferred | Not used for pyelonephritis (inadequate renal penetration) |

## Key Teaching Points for Case Design

1. **Collateral damage drives first-line cystitis selection, not just efficacy** — Nitrofurantoin and TMP/SMX are preferred not because they're more effective than FQs (efficacy is comparable at ~90-93%), but because FQs promote resistance and MRSA. A case selecting ciprofloxacin as first-line empiric cystitis therapy is wrong per these guidelines even if it "works."

2. **Fluoroquinolones are alternative, not preferred for cystitis** — The guideline explicitly says "reserve for important uses other than acute cystitis." This is the same message SJN echoes. Selecting FQ first-line for uncomplicated cystitis is an anti-pattern at both the national and local level.

3. **Pyelonephritis requires culture — always** — No empiric pyelonephritis treatment is complete without ordering urine culture. Cases that treat pyelonephritis without noting culture should flag this as an error.

4. **TMP/SMX for pyelonephritis: only if susceptible, and 14 days** — Empiric TMP/SMX is risky; add initial ceftriaxone. If used for known-susceptible organism, the guideline-supported duration is 14 days (vs. 7 days for FQ). A case giving TMP/SMX for 7 days for pyelonephritis is undertreating.

5. **Nitrofurantoin/fosfomycin cannot be used for pyelonephritis** — Bladder agents only; inadequate renal penetration. These are the classic "wrong" answers when a patient has systemic symptoms.

6. **20% TMP/SMX resistance threshold** — Below 20%, TMP/SMX is appropriate. Above 20%, switch to nitrofurantoin or fosfomycin first-line. This is a testable decision point — a case can present local antibiogram data that forces a choice.

7. **Amoxicillin/ampicillin should not be used empirically** — Very high worldwide resistance. A case prescribing amoxicillin empirically for cystitis is wrong unless Enterococcus is the confirmed pathogen.

8. **Beta-lactams are third-tier for cystitis** — Use only when nitrofurantoin, TMP/SMX, and FQs are all contraindicated. The high ESBL selection risk from broad-spectrum cephalosporins makes them particularly unattractive for a low-acuity infection.

9. **Postmenopausal women and diabetics without urological sequelae** — The 2010 guideline does not formally cover them, but notes "some experts" consider these uncomplicated. The 2025 IDSA cUTI guideline explicitly extends the uncomplicated classification to patients with DM or urologic abnormalities who are systemically well.

---

## Scope Limitations (relevant for case design)

- Covers: premenopausal, non-pregnant women without known urological abnormalities or comorbidities
- Does NOT cover: men, pregnant women, postmenopausal women (formally), recurrent UTI, UTI prevention, complicated UTI
- All pyelonephritis recommendations for outpatient mild-to-moderate disease; inpatient guidance is unchanged from 1999
