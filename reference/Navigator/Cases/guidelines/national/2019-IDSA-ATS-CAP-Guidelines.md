# 2019 ATS/IDSA Community-Acquired Pneumonia Guidelines

## Overview

16-question GRADE guideline addressing diagnosis, site of care, empiric therapy, and management of adults with CAP. Approved ATS May 2019, IDSA August 2019.

**Major departures from 2007 guideline:**
- HCAP category **abandoned** — does not predict drug-resistant pathogens; drove inappropriate broad-spectrum overuse without outcome benefit
- Emphasis on **locally validated risk factors** for MRSA and *P. aeruginosa* rather than global patient categories
- **De-escalation at 48h** when cultures are negative is now an explicit strong recommendation
- Routine follow-up chest imaging: **do not obtain** if improving at 5–7 days
- PCT: **do not use** to withhold initial antibiotics

---

## Severe CAP Definition (Table 1 — 2007 IDSA/ATS criteria, reaffirmed)

Severe CAP = **one major criterion OR three or more minor criteria**

| Major Criteria (either one = severe) |
|--------------------------------------|
| Septic shock requiring vasopressors |
| Respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation |

| Minor Criteria (need ≥3) |
|--------------------------|
| Respiratory rate >30 breaths/min |
| PaO₂/FiO₂ <250 |
| Multilobar infiltrates |
| Confusion/disorientation |
| BUN >20 mg/dL |
| WBC <4,000 cells/mL (due to infection, not chemotherapy) |
| Platelets <100,000/mL |
| Core temperature <36°C |
| Hypotension requiring aggressive IV fluid resuscitation |

## Diagnostic Questions

### Q1 — Sputum Culture

**Outpatient:** Do NOT obtain routinely (strong).

**Inpatient — obtain when:**
- Severe CAP, especially if intubated (strong)
- Empirically treating for MRSA or *P. aeruginosa* (strong)
- Prior MRSA or *P. aeruginosa* respiratory infection (conditional)
- Hospitalized + parenteral antibiotics in last 90 days (conditional)

**Rationale:** Negative cultures allow de-escalation; positive cultures confirm need for continued extended coverage.

### Q2 — Blood Cultures

**Outpatient:** Do NOT obtain (strong).

**Inpatient — do not routinely obtain** (conditional) **except when:**
- Severe CAP (strong)
- Empirically treating MRSA or *P. aeruginosa* (strong)
- Prior MRSA or *P. aeruginosa* (conditional)
- Hospitalized + parenteral abx in last 90 days (conditional)

### Q3 — Urinary Antigen Testing (Legionella + Pneumococcal)

- Do not routinely test (conditional)
- **Test in severe CAP** or epidemiologic Legionella exposure (conditional)

### Q4 — Influenza Testing

When influenza is circulating: **use rapid molecular assay (NAAT), preferred over antigen test** (strong).

### Q5 — Procalcitonin to Withhold Antibiotics

**Recommendation: START antibiotics regardless of PCT level** in confirmed CAP (strong, moderate evidence).

PCT cannot reliably distinguish viral from bacterial CAP. Sensitivity ranges 38–91% — insufficient to withhold therapy. Only exception: positive influenza + clinical stability + low PCT + no bacterial pathogen → may consider early discontinuation at 48–72h.

### Q6 — Site of Care (Outpatient vs. Inpatient)

Use **PSI (Pneumonia Severity Index)** as primary tool (strong, moderate evidence). CURB-65 acceptable but conditional recommendation.

PSI > CURB-65: identifies larger proportion of low-risk patients; higher discriminative power for 30-day mortality.

Clinical judgment always required — PSI cannot override inability to maintain oral intake, substance abuse, cognitive impairment, or severe comorbidities.

### Q7 — ICU vs. General Ward

- **Direct ICU admission** for vasopressors or mechanical ventilation (strong).
- Use **2007 IDSA/ATS minor severity criteria** (Table 1) to guide ICU triage (conditional).
- Patients transferred from ward to ICU have higher mortality than those admitted directly — don't under-triage.

## Treatment Questions

### Q8 — Outpatient Empiric Therapy (Table 3)

**No comorbidities, no MRSA/Pseudo risk:**
- Amoxicillin 1g TID (strong), OR
- Doxycycline 100mg BID (conditional), OR
- Macrolide — only if local pneumococcal resistance <25% (conditional)

**With comorbidities** (chronic heart/lung/liver/renal disease, DM, alcoholism, malignancy, asplenia):
- Beta-lactam (amox-clav 875/125 BID or cefpodoxime 200mg BID or cefuroxime 500mg BID) PLUS macrolide or doxycycline (strong for combination), OR
- Respiratory FQ monotherapy: levofloxacin 750mg, moxifloxacin 400mg, or gemifloxacin 320mg daily (strong)

---

### Q9.1 — Inpatient Non-Severe CAP (No MRSA/Pseudo Risk) (Table 4)

**Option 1 — Beta-lactam + macrolide (strong, HIGH evidence):**
- Beta-lactam: ampicillin-sulbactam 1.5–3g q6h, cefotaxime 1–2g q8h, **ceftriaxone 1–2g daily**, or ceftaroline 600mg q12h
- PLUS macrolide: **azithromycin 500mg daily** or clarithromycin 500mg BID

**Option 2 — Respiratory FQ monotherapy (strong, HIGH evidence):**
- Levofloxacin 750mg daily OR moxifloxacin 400mg daily

**Option 3 — Beta-lactam + doxycycline (conditional, LOW evidence):**
- Use only when macrolides AND FQs are contraindicated

**Do NOT use beta-lactam monotherapy** over these options.

---

### Q9.2 — Inpatient Severe CAP (No MRSA/Pseudo Risk)

**Option 1 — Beta-lactam + macrolide (strong, MODERATE evidence) — PREFERRED:**
- Same agents as Q9.1

**Option 2 — Beta-lactam + respiratory FQ (strong, LOW evidence):**
- Levofloxacin 750mg or moxifloxacin 400mg

**NOT recommended for severe CAP:**
- FQ monotherapy
- Beta-lactam + doxycycline

Observational data suggest macrolide-containing therapy associated with lower mortality in severe CAP (18% relative risk reduction vs. non-macrolide).

---

### Q10 — Aspiration Pneumonia: Anaerobic Coverage

**Recommendation: Do NOT routinely add anaerobic coverage** for suspected aspiration (conditional, very low evidence).

**Exception:** Add anaerobic coverage only if **lung abscess or empyema** is suspected.

**Rationale:** 1970s studies showing high anaerobe prevalence used trans-tracheal sampling late in disease — methodology biases. Modern data shows anaerobes are uncommon in hospitalized aspiration pneumonia. C. difficile risk from clindamycin/beta-lactam-inhibitor combos outweighs uncertain anaerobic benefit.

---

### Q11 — MRSA and *P. aeruginosa* Risk: Extended-Spectrum Therapy

**HCAP is ABANDONED.** Do not use HCAP designation to justify broad-spectrum therapy.

#### Risk Factor Framework

| Risk Factor | Strength |
|-------------|---------|
| Prior respiratory isolation of MRSA or *P. aeruginosa* | Strongest — justifies empiric coverage |
| Recent hospitalization + parenteral antibiotics in last 90 days | Second strongest — justifies cultures; empiric coverage for severe CAP only |
| All other published HCAP criteria (nursing home, dialysis, home infusion, etc.) | Weak — do NOT use alone to justify extended coverage |

#### Action by Severity and Risk

| Scenario | Non-Severe CAP | Severe CAP |
|----------|----------------|-----------|
| Prior MRSA respiratory isolation | Empiric MRSA coverage + cultures + nasal PCR | Empiric MRSA coverage + cultures |
| Prior Pseudo respiratory isolation | Cultures only, withhold Pseudo coverage unless cultures positive | Empiric Pseudo coverage + cultures |
| Recent hospitalization + parenteral abx (90d) + locally validated MRSA risk | Cultures + nasal PCR; withhold if PCR negative, add if positive | Empiric MRSA + cultures |
| Recent hospitalization + parenteral abx (90d) + locally validated Pseudo risk | Cultures only | Empiric Pseudo coverage + cultures |

**De-escalate at 48h** if cultures negative and patient improving — always.

#### Empiric MRSA Regimens
- Vancomycin 15mg/kg q12h (adjust to levels)
- Linezolid 600mg q12h

#### Empiric *P. aeruginosa* Regimens
- Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g q6h
- Cefepime 2g q8h
- Ceftazidime 2g q8h
- Aztreonam 2g q8h
- Meropenem 1g q8h
- Imipenem 500mg q6h

#### MRSA Nasal Swab (Rapid PCR)
- **Negative NPV >95%** — withhold MRSA coverage in non-severe CAP when negative
- **Positive** — start MRSA coverage + cultures; de-escalate if cultures negative

---

### Q12 — Corticosteroids

- **Do NOT routinely use** in non-severe CAP (strong, HIGH evidence)
- **Do NOT routinely use** in severe CAP (conditional, MODERATE evidence)
- **Do NOT use** in severe influenza pneumonia (conditional, LOW evidence)
- Exception: endorse Surviving Sepsis Campaign criteria for refractory septic shock
- Note: Steroids for COPD, asthma, or autoimmune comorbidities remain appropriate — guideline does not override disease-specific indications

---

### Q13 — Influenza: Antiviral Therapy

Treat influenza-positive CAP with **oseltamivir**, regardless of duration of illness before diagnosis:
- Inpatient: strong recommendation
- Outpatient: conditional recommendation

---

### Q14 — Influenza + CAP: Antibacterial Therapy

**Prescribe standard antibacterial therapy** even with positive influenza test (strong, LOW evidence).

Cannot exclude bacterial coinfection from influenza test alone. Up to 30% of influenza deaths had bacterial coinfection at autopsy. Common copathogens: *S. aureus*, *S. pneumoniae*, *H. influenzae*. Follow MRSA risk guidelines.

Exception: influenza-positive + clinical stability early + low PCT + no bacterial pathogen → may consider stopping antibacterial therapy at 48–72h.

---

### Q15 — Duration of Therapy

**Minimum 5 days**, guided by **clinical stability criteria** (strong, MODERATE evidence).

**Clinical stability criteria — all must be met:**
- Heart rate normal
- Respiratory rate ≤24
- Blood pressure stable (SBP ≥90)
- SpO₂ ≥90% on room air (or baseline supplemental O₂)
- Temperature ≤37.8°C (afebrile for ≥24h)
- Able to eat/take oral medications
- Normal mentation

**Failure to achieve stability by day 5:** assess for resistant pathogen, empyema, lung abscess, or alternative source.

Longer courses required only for: meningitis, endocarditis, other deep-seated infection, *Burkholderia pseudomallei*, TB, endemic fungi.

---

### Q16 — Follow-up Chest Imaging

**Do NOT routinely obtain follow-up chest imaging** if symptoms resolve within 5–7 days (conditional, LOW evidence).

Lung cancer screening remains appropriate per criteria (separate from routine CAP follow-up).

## Key Teaching Points for Case Design

1. **HCAP is wrong** — nursing home residence, dialysis, or home infusion do NOT automatically require anti-MRSA/Pseudo coverage. This was the 2005 paradigm; it is explicitly abandoned.
2. **90-day IV antibiotic exposure is the key risk factor** — not oral antibiotics, not short-course antibiotics, not outpatient antibiotics. Parenteral + hospitalization together.
3. **Prior respiratory isolation is stronger than hospitalization history** — if the organism was cultured from the respiratory tract, empiric coverage is warranted.
4. **De-escalation at 48h is mandatory, not optional** — coverage for MRSA or *P. aeruginosa* initiated empirically must be re-evaluated when cultures return.
5. **Aspiration ≠ anaerobic coverage** — the 2019 guideline (Q10) directly states: do not routinely add anaerobic coverage for suspected aspiration.
6. **Non-severe CAP with recent IV abx + known pathogen**: cultures first, withhold Pseudo empiric therapy, add only if cultures positive.
7. **Severe CAP with recent IV abx**: empiric extended-spectrum coverage + cultures + de-escalate.
8. **Minimum 5 days** — clinical stability drives stopping, not a fixed calendar. A case with clinical stability at day 3 still requires completion of 5 days.
9. **No routine follow-up imaging** — if resolving at 5–7 days, no CXR needed.
10. **MRSA nasal swab utility**: negative has excellent NPV for de-escalation; positive mandates cultures but doesn't guarantee pneumonia.
