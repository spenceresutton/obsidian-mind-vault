# 2023 IWGDF/IDSA DFI Guidelines

Senneville É, Albalawi Z, van Asten SA, et al. *IWGDF/IDSA Guidelines on the Diagnosis and Treatment of Diabetes-related Foot Infections (IWGDF/IDSA 2023).* Clin Infect Dis. 2023;ciad527.

This national guideline supplies the **reasoning** behind DFI case calibration. The empiric regimen tables that govern SJN case correctness live in [[reference/Navigator/Cases/guidelines/local/SJN-SSTI-Guidelines#Diabetic Foot Infection (DFI)]] (see [[brain/Case Style Guide#Guideline Hierarchy]]).

## Diagnosis & Classification

- **Diagnose DFI clinically** based on local or systemic signs and symptoms of inflammation (Rec 1a; strong, low). Signs may be masked by neuropathy, PAD, or immune dysfunction.
- **Assess severity using the IWGDF/IDSA classification scheme** (Rec 1b; strong, low). Mild / moderate / severe; bone involvement staged 3-O or 4-O.
- Assess inflammatory serum biomarkers (CRP, ESR, or PCT) when clinical exam is equivocal (Rec 3).
- Send a **tissue specimen by curettage or biopsy** for culture (Rec 5; conditional, moderate). Surface swabs are unreliable. Use conventional, not molecular, microbiology for first-line identification (Rec 6).
- **Osteomyelitis:** combine probe-to-bone, plain X-ray, and ESR/CRP/PCT as initial workup (Rec 7). MRI when diagnosis remains in doubt (Rec 8). Obtain bone (not soft tissue) samples for culture when osteomyelitis is suspected (Rec 10).

## Antibiotic Selection & Targeting

- **Do not treat clinically uninfected ulcers** to reduce infection risk or promote healing (Rec 11; best practice).
- **Mild DFI, North America / Western Europe, no recent antibiotics:** target aerobic gram-positives only — beta-hemolytic streptococci and *S. aureus* (including MRSA if indicated) (Rec 14; best practice).
- **Do NOT empirically target *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* in temperate climates** (Rec 15). Cover *P. aeruginosa* only if isolated within the previous few weeks, or in moderate/severe infection in a person residing in Asia or North Africa.
- Select agents by likely/proven pathogen, susceptibility, severity, evidence, collateral flora damage, drug interactions, availability, and cost (Rec 13).

## Duration

- **Soft tissue DFI: 1–2 weeks** (Rec 12b; strong, high).
- Consider up to 3–4 weeks if infection is extensive and resolving slower than expected, or with severe PAD (Rec 12c).
- Re-evaluate if infection has not resolved after 4 weeks of apparently appropriate therapy (Rec 12d).
- **Osteomyelitis (Rec 16):** up to 3 weeks after minor amputation with positive bone margin; **6 weeks** for osteomyelitis without bone resection or amputation. Consider antibiotic-only treatment for forefoot osteomyelitis without need for I&D, without PAD, and without exposed bone (Rec 22).

## Surgery & Vascular

- Hospitalize for severe foot infection, or moderate infection with key morbidities (Rec 2).
- **Urgent surgical consult** for severe infection or moderate DFI complicated by extensive gangrene, necrotizing infection, deep (below fascia) abscess, compartment syndrome, or severe limb ischaemia (Rec 18).
- Consider early (within 24–48 h) surgery with antibiotics for moderate/severe DFIs to remove infected and necrotic tissue (Rec 19).
- **Urgent vascular consult** for PAD with infected foot ulcer or gangrene — revascularization may be required (Rec 20).
- An experienced ID specialist/team should evaluate severe DFI within 24 h.

## What NOT to Do

- No adjunctive G-CSF; no topical antiseptics, silver, honey, bacteriophage, or negative-pressure wound therapy as infection treatment (Rec 23).
- No topical antibiotics combined with systemic antibiotics for soft tissue or bone infection (Rec 24).
- No hyperbaric or topical oxygen therapy for the sole indication of treating a DFI (Rec 25).
