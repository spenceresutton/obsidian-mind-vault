# 2010 IDSA CAUTI Guidelines

## Definitions — The Threshold Distinction That Matters

### CA-UTI (symptomatic)
- Signs or symptoms of UTI with no other identified source of infection
- **PLUS ≥10³ CFU/mL** of ≥1 bacterial species from a single catheter urine specimen OR midstream voided specimen collected within 48h of catheter removal
- Note: lower threshold than the standard 10⁵ CFU/mL used in non-catheterized patients — catheter specimens are less likely to be contaminated, so lower counts represent true bacteriuria

### CA-ASB (asymptomatic)
- **≥10⁵ CFU/mL** of ≥1 bacterial species from a catheter specimen WITHOUT symptoms
- Same threshold as non-catheterized ASB

**The clinical relevance:** A catheterized patient with 10⁴ CFU/mL and symptoms of UTI meets the CA-UTI definition. A catheterized patient with 10⁴ CFU/mL and no symptoms does NOT meet CA-ASB criteria — it's below the 10⁵ threshold. This distinction is deliberately asymmetric because it reflects that low counts in symptomatic catheterized patients are significant, while high counts in asymptomatic catheterized patients are common and do not warrant treatment.

### Symptoms Compatible with CA-UTI

In catheterized patients:
- New onset or worsening: fever, rigors, altered mental status, malaise, or lethargy (no other cause)
- Flank pain or costovertebral angle tenderness
- Acute hematuria
- Pelvic discomfort

After catheter removal:
- Dysuria, urgency, frequency, or suprapubic pain/tenderness

In SCI patients specifically:
- Increased spasticity, autonomic dysreflexia, or sense of unease also qualify

## What Does NOT Indicate CA-UTI or Treatment

**Pyuria:**
- Presence, absence, or degree of pyuria does NOT differentiate CA-ASB from CA-UTI (A-II)
- Pyuria accompanying CA-ASB is NOT an indication for antimicrobial treatment (A-II)
- Absence of pyuria in a symptomatic patient suggests a diagnosis OTHER than CA-UTI (A-III)

**Urine appearance:**
- Odorous or cloudy urine alone does NOT indicate CA-ASB vs. CA-UTI, does NOT warrant urine culture, and does NOT warrant antimicrobial therapy (A-III)

**Positive culture alone:**
- CA-ASB should not be screened for or treated except in pregnant women and selected research contexts (A-III)
- Bacteriuria in catheterized patients is nearly universal with prolonged catheterization — a positive culture without symptoms is not CA-UTI

## Treatment

### Step 1: Culture Before Treating

Always obtain urine culture before initiating antimicrobial therapy for presumed CA-UTI (A-III):
- Wide spectrum of potential organisms
- High likelihood of antimicrobial resistance in catheterized patients
- CA-UTI is often polymicrobial and MDR — empiric therapy must be confirmed by culture

### Step 2: Catheter Management

**If catheter has been in place ≥2 weeks at CA-UTI onset AND is still required:**
- Replace the catheter (A-I) — reduces polymicrobial bacteriuria at 28 days, shortens time to clinical improvement, lowers re-infection rate
- Obtain culture from the freshly placed catheter, not the old one — biofilm on the old catheter does not accurately reflect bladder infection status

**If catheter can be discontinued:**
- Remove it; collect midstream voided urine for culture before starting antibiotics (A-III)

### Step 3: Antibiotic Selection

No specific empiric antibiotic recommendation is made in this guideline — coverage depends on:
- Local resistance data / antibiogram
- Prior cultures from the patient
- Suspected organism based on clinical context

**Important exclusion: Moxifloxacin** — do NOT use for UTI. Urinary concentrations are uncertain and potentially inadequate.

**Adjust to culture results** as soon as susceptibilities are available. De-escalate when possible — catheterized patients with MDR organisms are a reservoir for cross-infection.

### Step 4: Duration

| Clinical scenario | Duration |
|-------------------|----------|
| Prompt symptom resolution | 7 days (A-III) |
| Delayed response | 10–14 days (A-III) |
| Non-severely ill, levofloxacin | 5 days (B-III) |
| Women ≤65 years, lower tract CA-UTI, catheter removed | 3 days (B-II) |

Duration applies regardless of whether the patient remains catheterized.

**Extend and evaluate urology if:** no defervescence by 72h of appropriate therapy. Consider whether the diagnosis is correct, source control is adequate, or a complication (abscess, obstruction) is present.

## CA-ASB at Catheter Removal — Edge Case

**Women with CA-ASB persisting ≥48h after short-term catheter removal:**
- Treatment may be considered to reduce subsequent CA-UTI (C-I — weak recommendation, low evidence)

**Men:**
- No recommendation for or against screening or treating persistent CA-ASB after catheter removal — insufficient evidence

This is the weakest treatment-adjacent recommendation in the guideline. The evidence grade is C-I, meaning expert opinion with class I data is lacking. SJN alignment: SJN does not recommend routine treatment of CA-ASB at catheter removal for either sex.

## Microbiology of CA-UTI

**Short-term catheter (<30 days):** Predominantly gram-negative (E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas, Proteus, Serratia, Enterobacter), Enterococcus

**Long-term catheter (≥30 days):** Typically polymicrobial. MDR organisms predominate. Gram-positive cocci (coagulase-negative staph, Enterococcus) increasingly represented.

**Funguria (candiduria):** Reported in 3–32% of long-term catheterized patients. Fungal UTI treatment is addressed in the IDSA candidiasis guideline, not this one.

**Key stewardship point:** CA-ASB is a large reservoir of MDR uropathogens. Treating CA-ASB selects for resistance and does not reduce CA-UTI — it amplifies the MDR problem. The primary stewardship intervention is catheter removal, not antibiotics.

## Prevention Hierarchy

### Most Impactful: Restrict and Remove Catheters

1. **Only insert when indicated** (A-III). Do not catheterize for staff convenience.
2. **Remove as soon as no longer required** (A-I for bacteriuria risk, A-II for CA-UTI risk) — the single most effective prevention strategy
3. **Maintain closed drainage system** — do not disconnect catheter-tubing junction; port for needle aspiration only (A-II/A-III)

### Alternative Catheterization Methods (when feasible)

- **Intermittent catheterization:** preferred over indwelling for patients who need bladder drainage but have intermittent need (A-II)
- **Condom catheter:** preferred in men with adequate bladder emptying vs. indwelling urethral catheter (A-II)
- **Suprapubic catheter:** evidence is extrapolated from urethral catheter literature; reasonable alternative when long-term drainage required

### Strategies That Do NOT Work

| Strategy | Evidence | Verdict |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Systemic antibiotic prophylaxis (short or long-term) | A-III | Do not use — selects for resistance |
| Antimicrobial catheter irrigation | A-II | Do not use |
| Antibiotics added to drainage bag | A-I | Do not use |
| Prophylactic antibiotics at catheter removal | B-I | Do not use routinely |
| Prophylactic antibiotics at catheter replacement | A-III | Do not use |
| Routine catheter change to prevent bacteriuria | insufficient evidence | No recommendation |
| Cranberry products in catheterized patients | negative data | Do not use routinely |
| Meatal cleansing with antiseptics/antimicrobials | no benefit | Do not use |

## SJN vs. National CAUTI Comparison

| Scenario | IDSA 2010 | SJN |
|----------|-----------|-----|
| CA-ASB — catheterized patient, asymptomatic | Do not treat | Do not treat; catheter removal is the intervention |
| CA-UTI — systemic symptoms | Culture + treat 7 days | Culture + treat; remove/replace catheter |
| CA-UTI — catheter still needed | Replace catheter first, culture from new catheter, treat | Remove/replace catheter; antibiotics for systemic symptoms |
| Pyuria alone in catheterized patient | Not an indication for treatment | Not an indication for treatment |
| Odor/cloudiness alone | Not an indication for culture or treatment | Not an indication for treatment |
| Duration | 7d (prompt resolution); 10-14d (delayed) | 7 days (aligned) |

**Key SJN divergence note:** SJN operationalizes CAUTI management as "catheter removal is the primary intervention." Antibiotics are triggered by systemic symptoms (fever, hemodynamic instability), not positive culture. This is more operationalized than the national guideline but fully consistent with its principles.

## Key Teaching Points for Case Design

1. **Pyuria in a catheterized patient is not CA-UTI** — This is the most common over-treatment trigger. A case presenting a catheterized patient with pyuria and no symptoms is testing whether the candidate initiates antibiotics. Wrong answer. Pyuria does not differentiate CA-ASB from CA-UTI (A-II).

2. **Odor and cloudiness are not diagnostic** — A case presenting "foul-smelling" or cloudy urine in a catheterized patient is explicitly testing this anti-pattern. Per both SJN and national guidelines, odor/cloudiness alone does NOT warrant culture or treatment. This is also why foul-smell language is flagged as a case anti-pattern in the Style Guide.

3. **CA-UTI threshold is 10³, not 10⁵** — In a symptomatic catheterized patient, 10³ CFU/mL meets the definition. A case presenting 10⁴ CFU/mL with symptoms should be recognized as CA-UTI, not "low-count bacteriuria requiring repeat culture."

4. **Culture before treating — always** — CA-UTI organisms are polymicrobial, MDR, and locally variable. Empiric therapy without culture is a stewardship failure. Cases should include an order for urine culture as part of the correct management.

5. **Replace the catheter if it's been in ≥2 weeks** — A case where the catheter has been in place for weeks and the patient develops CA-UTI: replace first, culture from the new catheter, then treat. Treating without replacing will show worse outcomes (less clearance, faster recurrence).

6. **Catheter removal is the primary intervention** — A case with CAUTI where the catheter is no longer clinically necessary: the answer is remove the catheter, obtain culture, then determine if antibiotics are needed based on systemic symptoms. Reflexive antibiotics without catheter removal misses the primary intervention.

7. **Do not use moxifloxacin for UTI** — Urinary concentrations are uncertain. A case offering moxifloxacin as an oral option for CA-UTI step-down is a trap.

8. **Do not treat CA-ASB to prevent CA-UTI** — It doesn't work. The evidence shows treatment of CA-ASB in catheterized patients selects for resistance without reducing CA-UTI incidence. A case presenting asymptomatic bacteriuria in a long-term catheterized patient and asking for treatment optimization: withhold antibiotics, consider catheter replacement.

9. **Duration 7 days for improving patients** — 14-day courses for CA-UTI are over-treatment in patients with prompt symptom resolution. SJN and 2010 IDSA agree on 7 days.

10. **Long-term catheter = polymicrobial MDR risk** — A case with a patient on a long-term Foley presenting with CA-UTI: expect polymicrobial coverage needs, always culture-guided. Empiric narrow-spectrum therapy for a long-term catheterized patient is likely to be inadequate.

---

## Scope Limitations

- Covers: short-term (<30d) and long-term (≥30d) indwelling urethral catheters, suprapubic catheters, intermittent catheterization, condom catheters
- Does NOT cover: single in-and-out catheterization, ureteral stents, nephrostomy tubes, fungal UTI
- Fungal UTI: refer to IDSA candidiasis guidelines
