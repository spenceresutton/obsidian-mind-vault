# Kulkarni 2026 CID — Bacterial Prostatitis State-of-the-Art Review

## Disease Classification

**Acute Bacterial Prostatitis (ABP)**
- Acute presentation: fever, dysuria, perineal/pelvic pain, ± bacteremia
- Diagnosis: acute UTI syndrome + systemic illness + prostatic tenderness or fluctuance on DRE, OR prostatic abscess on imaging
- DRE: brief examination encouraged; vigorous prostatic massage explicitly discouraged (bacteremia risk)

**Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis (CBP)**
- Presentation: recurrent UTIs or chronic pelvic pain symptoms ≥3 months
- Diagnosis requires Meares-Stamey localization testing (2-glass or 4-glass):
  - **4-glass test** (Meares-Stamey): VB1 (urethral) → VB2 (midstream) → EPS (expressed prostatic secretions) → VB3 (post-massage)
  - **2-glass test (simplified):** Pre-massage midstream urine → post-massage first-void
  - **Positive criteria:** ≥10-fold increase in CFU in post-massage specimen vs. pre-massage, OR uropathogen grows post-massage with negative pre-massage
  - **Critical timing rule:** Do NOT perform within 72 hours of ejaculation — false negative results

**Why the distinction matters for cases:**
- ABP: labeled as complicated UTI only for treatment logistics; duration implications differ from CBP
- CBP: blood-prostate barrier is intact — antibiotic selection is driven by tissue penetration, not just in vitro susceptibility

## Microbiology by Type

### ABP

| Organism | Frequency |
|----------|-----------|
| *E. coli* | 68% |
| *Klebsiella spp.* | ~8% |
| *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* | ~6% |
| *Proteus spp.* | ~5% |
| *Enterococcus spp.* | ~4% |

*S. aureus* isolation in prostatitis → suspect hematogenous seeding from a distant source (e.g., skin, endocarditis). Work up the primary source.

### CBP

| Organism | Frequency |
|----------|-----------|
| *Enterococcus spp.* | 41% (predominant) |
| *E. coli* | 17% |
| *Klebsiella spp.* | ~8% |

**Key shift:** CBP is NOT enterobacterales-dominated. Enterococcus predominance is the major distinction from ABP and from uncomplicated UTI. This affects empiric coverage decisions for CBP patients on long-term suppressive therapy.

## Antibiotic Penetration into Prostatic Tissue

### The Blood-Prostate Barrier

The prostate is supplied by nonfenestrated capillaries. In the healthy state, this limits penetration of hydrophilic agents. In ABP, inflammation disrupts this barrier — broadening effective antibiotic options empirically. In CBP, the barrier is intact.

| Drug Class | Penetration | Mechanism | Appropriate for ABP | Appropriate for CBP |
|------------|-------------|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Fluoroquinolones | Excellent | Lipid-soluble; diffuses across intact barrier | Yes | Yes (preferred) |
| TMP/SMX | Good | Sufficient lipid solubility | Yes | Yes (second-line) |
| Beta-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins) | Poor in CBP; acceptable in ABP | Hydrophilic; relies on inflammation to open barrier | Acceptable empirically | NOT preferred for definitive CBP therapy |
| Aminoglycosides | Poor | Hydrophilic; inadequate penetration even with inflammation | Adjunct only | No |
| Fosfomycin | Variable; evidence emerging | Achieves detectable prostatic levels | Off-label option | Emerging evidence for refractory CBP |

**Practical implication:** A patient with CBP caused by a susceptible organism who receives cefpodoxime may have culture-documented eradication in urine but persistent prostatic infection — explaining relapse. This is NOT a sensitivity testing failure; it is a penetration failure.

## Empiric Treatment

### ABP (IV phase)

**Preferred empiric regimens:**
- Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g q8h IV
- Third-generation cephalosporin (ceftriaxone 1–2g IV daily)
- Fluoroquinolone IV (ciprofloxacin 400mg q12h)

**IV-to-oral step-down** (after clinical improvement — fever resolution, tolerating PO):
- Fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin 500mg BID or levofloxacin 500–750mg daily) — preferred
- TMP/SMX DS 1 tablet BID — appropriate if susceptible

**Minimum total duration:** 2 weeks. Duration is counted from initiation of effective therapy.

**Special case — bacteremia:** S. aureus bacteremia in the setting of prostatitis warrants evaluation for endocarditis and hematogenous source. Standard UTI blood culture guidance does not apply — treat as bacteremia investigation.

**Urinary retention + ABP:** If urinary retention develops, suprapubic catheter preferred over transurethral Foley. Passing a catheter through acutely inflamed prostatic urethra is painful and risks bacteremia. If Foley is unavoidable, consider a Coudé catheter to navigate the prostatic urethra.

---

### CBP

**First-line:**
- Fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin 500mg BID or levofloxacin 500–750mg daily) — 6 weeks
  - Largest evidence base; achieves sustained prostatic tissue levels

**Alternative:**
- TMP/SMX DS 1 tablet BID — 6 weeks
  - Lower penetration than FQs but acceptable for susceptible organisms

**Refractory CBP:**
- Repeat 6-week course of FQ or TMP/SMX, OR
- Extend to 12 weeks, OR
- Switch to alternate class

**Emerging option (off-label):**
- Fosfomycin 3g PO q24–48h × 2–12 weeks
  - Evidence from small series; reasonable option for FQ-resistant or FQ-intolerant CBP

**Enterococcus-specific:**
- Enterococcus is the most common CBP pathogen. FQs are active against most enterococcal isolates but not vancomycin-resistant enterococcus (VRE). Confirm susceptibility. Amoxicillin is an option for susceptible *E. faecalis* if FQ is contraindicated.

## Prostatic Abscess

**Non-operative management appropriate if:**
- Abscess ≤1–2 cm
- Single collection (not multi-loculated)
- Clinically responding on appropriate antibiotics
- Follow-up imaging feasible to confirm resolution

**Surgical drainage (transurethral or transrectal):**
- Refractory or not responding after 48–72h of antibiotics
- Larger collections or multi-loculated abscess
- Clinical deterioration

## Diagnosis Workup Notes

**PSA:** Do NOT use PSA for prostatitis diagnosis or as a decision point for empiric therapy. PSA rises with prostatitis of any cause and does not distinguish bacterial from non-bacterial etiology. It is useful for tracking post-treatment resolution but not for diagnosis.

**Urine culture:** Always required. Blood cultures for bacteremic presentation. Meares-Stamey localization for CBP diagnosis.

**Imaging:** Transrectal ultrasound (TRUS) or pelvic CT if abscess suspected. MRI for complex or recurrent cases.

## Key Teaching Points for Case Design

1. **ABP ≠ cUTI for duration purposes** — Labeling ABP as "complicated UTI" and defaulting to a 7-day course is wrong. ABP requires a minimum of 2 weeks regardless of clinical recovery trajectory. The 2025 IDSA cUTI duration guidance explicitly carves out suspected prostatitis as requiring 10–14 days, but the prostatitis literature supports 2 weeks as the minimum for ABP specifically.

2. **CBP anti-pattern: beta-lactam for definitive therapy** — A case showing CBP caused by a susceptible organism with a culture-guided switch to cefpodoxime is testing whether candidates recognize the penetration problem. The bug is sensitive in vitro; the prostate is not the bladder.

3. **Meares-Stamey timing error** — Performing the test within 72 hours of ejaculation produces false-negative results. A case can present a "negative" localization test in this context; the correct answer is to repeat after 72-hour abstinence, not to conclude no prostatic source.

4. **Enterococcus in CBP is the leading organism** — Cases that present CBP with Enterococcus should not be treated like gram-negative UTI. FQ susceptibility must be confirmed. A case selecting ceftriaxone for CBP caused by susceptible *E. faecalis* is wrong twice: wrong penetration (barrier intact) and narrow-spectrum concern (Enterococcus has intrinsic resistance to many cephalosporins).

5. **S. aureus prostatitis signals hematogenous source** — A case presenting S. aureus on prostatic culture should trigger source investigation, not just a 6-week FQ course. The teaching point is pattern recognition for hematogenous seeding.

6. **DRE: brief yes, vigorous massage no** — A vigorous DRE or prostatic massage in ABP can precipitate bacteremia. Cases can test this by presenting a clinical scenario where a provider "thoroughly massaged" the prostate.

7. **Foley vs. Coudé catheter in acute retention** — If a patient with ABP needs decompression, transurethral Foley risks bacteremic trauma to the inflamed urethra. Suprapubic catheter is preferred. A case can present acute urinary retention with ABP and test whether the candidate selects suprapubic drainage.

8. **PSA is a distractor, not a diagnostic tool** — A case can include an elevated PSA in the lab panel as a distractor. Candidates who treat PSA as diagnostic confirmation or who pursue biopsy are following the anti-pattern.

9. **FQ for 4 weeks is not enough for CBP** — A 4-week course is below guideline recommendations. Six weeks is the minimum. Cases can test truncated courses.

10. **Blood cultures in ABP bacteremia** — Bacteremia occurs in ABP but does not automatically require blood culture investigation beyond clinical judgment, UNLESS S. aureus is isolated (hematogenous source workup warranted). Reflexively ordering bone scans, echo, or extended gram-negative bacteremia workup is over-investigation. This is a stewardship teaching point about not over-extending the bacteremia framework into routine uro-bacteremia.
