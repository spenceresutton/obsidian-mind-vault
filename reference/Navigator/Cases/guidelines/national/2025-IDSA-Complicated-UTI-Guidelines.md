# 2025 IDSA Complicated UTI Guidelines

## Status Note

CQ1 (empiric antibiotic selection), CQ2 (IV-to-oral switch), and CQ3 (duration) are fully ingested below. CQ4 (imaging) is in a separate companion paper not yet ingested.

---

## Why This Guideline Was Updated

The 2010 IDSA guideline addressed only uncomplicated cystitis and pyelonephritis in women. Key drivers for the 2025 update:

1. **Scope gap** — No prior IDSA guideline addressed complicated UTI or UTI in men
2. **Resistance** — Inpatient Enterobacterales resistance (2018–2020): FQ 27.5%, TMP/SMX 25.4%, nitrofurantoin 27% — all exceed 2010 thresholds
3. **New drugs** — Multiple FDA-approved cUTI agents since 2010 requiring evidence-based integration
4. **Classification misalignment** — Prior cUTI/uUTI definitions were anatomy-based; updated to severity-based

---

## Updated Classifications

**Complicated UTI** = signs or symptoms suggesting infection extending BEYOND the bladder:
- Fever, chills, rigors, or hemodynamic instability
- Flank pain or costovertebral angle tenderness
- Pyelonephritis is cUTI
- UTI with systemic symptoms associated with urinary catheter

**Uncomplicated UTI** = bladder-confined:
- Dysuria, urgency, frequency, suprapubic pain only
- No fever, no systemic signs, no flank pain/CVA tenderness

**Critical 2025 departure:** DM, immunocompromise, and underlying urologic abnormalities (BPH, cystocele) do NOT automatically = complicated UTI. Classification follows clinical presentation, not host risk factors.

**Prostatitis:** Must be excluded before classifying febrile UTI in men as uncomplicated. Prostatitis, epididymitis, and orchitis are outside scope of these guidelines.

## CQ1: Empiric Antibiotic Selection for cUTI

### Four-Step Framework

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Assess severity of illness (sepsis vs. no sepsis) |
| 2 | Evaluate patient-specific risk factors for resistant uropathogens |
| 3 | Evaluate patient-specific practical factors (allergies, contraindications, drug interactions) |
| 4 | For septic patients only: consult local antibiogram |

**Antibiogram thresholds (Step 4):**
- Septic shock: use antibiogram threshold >90% susceptible
- Sepsis without shock: use threshold >80% susceptible
- No sepsis: antibiogram not formally required

**Resistance risk factors (Step 2):**
- Prior urine culture with resistant organism
- Fluoroquinolone exposure in the past 12 months

---

### Empiric Antibiotic Options by Severity

#### With Sepsis (including septic shock)

**Preferred:** Third/fourth-generation cephalosporins, carbapenems, piperacillin-tazobactam, or fluoroquinolones

**Avoid as first-line:** Newer agents (novel BLBLIs, cefiderocol, plazomicin) and older aminoglycosides

- Carbapenems are appropriate for septic patients but are NOT default first-line — stewardship priority; escalate based on resistance risk factors and antibiogram
- ESBL-producing organism in prior cultures → ceftriaxone and pip/tazo are inappropriate; carbapenem preferred
- FQ exposure in past 12 months OR prior FQ-resistant culture → avoid FQ empirically

#### Without Sepsis (IV)

**Preferred:** Third/fourth-generation cephalosporins, piperacillin-tazobactam, or fluoroquinolones

**Avoid:** Carbapenems (not needed without sepsis), newer agents, older aminoglycosides

#### Without Sepsis (oral route appropriate)

**Options:** TMP/SMX, fluoroquinolones, or oral cephalosporins (see dosing table below)

**Avoid:** Nitrofurantoin and oral fosfomycin — do NOT achieve adequate tissue levels in systemic infection or renal parenchyma

---

| Ceftazidime-avibactam | 2.5g (over 2h) q8h |

### Oral Dosing Reference (Table 3.1 — for IV-to-oral step-down)

| Drug | Dose (normal renal function) | Notes |
|------|------------------------------|-------|
| Levofloxacin | 500–750mg daily | Highly bioavailable; preferred oral step-down |
| Ciprofloxacin | 500–750mg q12h | Highly bioavailable |
| TMP/SMX | 800/160mg q12h | Effective if susceptible |
| Amoxicillin-clavulanate | 875/125mg q8–12h | Inferior outcomes vs. FQ/TMP-SMX; use with ID pharmacist |
| Cephalexin | 500–1000mg q6h | Inferior outcomes; use with ID pharmacist if no alternatives |
| Cefpodoxime | 200–400mg q12h | Third-gen oral ceph; may be comparable to FQ/TMP-SMX for step-down |
| Cefixime | 400mg daily | Third-gen oral ceph |
| Ceftibuten | 400mg daily | Only oral beta-lactam with modern RCT data; inferior to norfloxacin for relapse |

**Nitrofurantoin and oral fosfomycin: NOT appropriate for cUTI step-down** — inadequate renal parenchymal penetration.

---

### Agent-Specific Panel Notes

**Ceftriaxone:** Preferred empiric choice for non-septic cUTI. Comparable to ertapenem in RCTs (clinical cure ~87–88%). Rising ESBL prevalence is the main threat to its role.

**Piperacillin-tazobactam:** Preferred for non-septic cUTI; can be used in septic patients if antibiogram supports it. Lower microbiologic eradication than comparators in RCTs (60.8% vs. 74.2%), though similar clinical cure. Rising ESBL and MDR-Pseudo challenging its effectiveness. Extended/continuous infusion increasingly used.

**Fluoroquinolones:** Preferred for non-septic cUTI. Excellent oral bioavailability — IV-to-oral switch is driven by ability to take oral medications, not bioavailability concerns. Avoid if FQ exposure in past 12 months or prior FQ-resistant culture.

**Carbapenems:** Preferred for septic cUTI, especially with ESBL risk. NOT first-line for non-septic cUTI — stewardship imperative to preserve carbapenem effectiveness. Ertapenem once-daily and available IM; meropenem/imipenem for broader gram-negative or high-acuity coverage.

**Older aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin):** Not preferred empirically — nephrotoxicity risk; acceptable for culture-directed therapy in specific situations (e.g., single-dose aminoglycoside for uncomplicated cystitis per SJN protocol).

**Nitrofurantoin/fosfomycin (oral):** Bladder-only agents. Inappropriate for cUTI — cannot achieve adequate systemic or renal tissue levels.

## CQ2: IV-to-Oral Switch for cUTI

### Recommendations

**Recommendation I — Non-bacteremic cUTI (including pyelonephritis):**
In patients treated initially with parenteral therapy who are clinically improving, able to take oral medication, and for whom an effective oral option is available → switch to oral rather than continuing IV for the remaining duration (conditional, low evidence).

**Recommendation II — Bacteremic cUTI (gram-negative bacteremia):**
In patients clinically improving, able to take oral medication, and for whom an effective oral option is available → switch to oral rather than continuing IV (conditional, very low evidence).
- Trials required patients to be afebrile and hemodynamically stable at time of switch
- Source control (relief of urinary obstruction) must be achieved before switching

---

### Five Criteria for IV-to-Oral Switch Eligibility

1. Patient is clinically improving
2. Source control achieved (if applicable — urinary obstruction relieved)
3. Patient can absorb oral antibiotics (no GI barrier)
4. Effective oral option exists for the causative pathogen (adequate levels in urine, tissue, and bloodstream if bacteremia)
5. No psychosocial or economic factors favoring the IV route

**"Effective oral agent"** means the drug achieves therapeutic levels in the relevant compartments AND is active against the pathogen. Clinical improvement on ceftriaxone → switch to oral 3rd-gen cephalosporin is reasonable. Oral bioavailability alone is not sufficient — susceptibility must also be confirmed or reasonably inferred.

---

### GI Contraindications to Oral Switch

Withhold oral switch if any of the following are present:
- Malabsorption syndrome
- Short bowel syndrome
- Ileus
- Severe diarrhea
- Significant vomiting
- Motility disorders
- Delayed gastric emptying
- Shock with reduced gut perfusion

**Drug interactions:** Calcium, iron, magnesium supplements, and sucralfate reduce FQ bioavailability. Food affects absorption of some agents. Time oral doses appropriately.

---

### Oral Agent Selection for Step-Down

**Preferred:**
| Drug | Oral Absorption | Urinary Excretion | Dose |
|------|----------------|-------------------|------|
| Levofloxacin | 99% | 64–100% | 500–750mg daily |
| Ciprofloxacin | 70% | 40–50% | 500–750mg q12h |
| TMP/SMX | 70–90% | 84% (SMX) | 800/160mg q12h |

**3rd-gen oral cephalosporins (acceptable; use when FQ/TMP-SMX unavailable or resistant):**
| Drug | Oral Absorption | Urinary Excretion | Dose |
|------|----------------|-------------------|------|
| Cefpodoxime | 50% | 80% | 200–400mg q12h |
| Cefixime | 50% | 50% | 400mg daily |
| Ceftibuten | 75–90% | 73% | 400mg daily or 200mg q12h |
| Cefuroxime | 52% | 90% | 500mg q12h |

Observational data suggest 3rd-gen oral cephalosporins may be comparable to FQ/TMP-SMX for step-down, including in bacteremic cUTI. Evidence is not from RCTs.

**Use with ID pharmacist consultation (inferior outcomes data):**
| Drug | Oral Absorption | Urinary Excretion | Dose | Note |
|------|----------------|-------------------|------|------|
| Cephalexin | 90% | 90% | 500–1000mg q6h | Multiple studies show inferior outcomes vs. FQ/TMP-SMX |
| Amoxicillin-clavulanate | 80% | 50–70% | 875/125mg q8–12h | Variable clavulanate absorption; inferior outcomes |

**Do NOT use for cUTI step-down:**
- **Nitrofurantoin** — inadequate tissue/bloodstream levels; bladder only
- **Oral fosfomycin** — inadequate tissue/bloodstream levels for systemic infection; small study data exists but not confirmed in larger RCTs
- **Cefdinir** — only 25% oral absorption and 13–23% urinary excretion; readmission rates higher vs. FQ/TMP-SMX in retrospective data
- **Amoxicillin, cefadroxil, cefaclor** — insufficient data for cUTI; not recommended

**High-dose oral beta-lactam note:** Some institutions use higher doses for step-down (amoxicillin 1000mg q8h, amox-clav 875–1000mg q8h, cephalexin 1000mg q6h) based on retrospective bacteremia data showing equivalence to 14 days of therapy when using these doses. Standard doses may not achieve adequate urinary levels.

**Bacteremia-specific:** The oral step-down agent must achieve therapeutic levels in bloodstream — not just urine. This narrows options more than for non-bacteremic cUTI. 3rd-gen oral cephalosporins with good absorption (cefpodoxime, ceftibuten) are reasonable; cephalexin and amox-clav are weaker choices.

**Renal insufficiency:** Consult pharmacist for severe cases — some agents have delayed clearance, others are accelerated by renal replacement therapy.

---

### Timing of Switch

Evidence from RCTs: switch occurred on day 3–4 when clinical parameters were improving. No data mandate a specific day — clinical eligibility (the five criteria above) drives timing, not a fixed day number.

---

### Stewardship Value of Early Switch

- Earlier discharge reduces nosocomial pathogen exposure (C. diff, resistant organisms)
- Avoids PICC/central line complications
- Reduces costs vs. OPAT
- Oral antibiotics are not inferior to IV antibiotics when bioavailability is equivalent and the pathogen is susceptible

## CQ3: Duration of Antibiotics for cUTI

### Recommendations

**Non-bacteremic cUTI** (including pyelonephritis), clinically improving on effective therapy:
- Fluoroquinolone: **5–7 days** (conditional, moderate evidence) vs. 10–14 days
- Non-fluoroquinolone: **7 days** (conditional, very low evidence) vs. 10–14 days

**Bacteremic cUTI**, clinically improving on effective therapy:
- **7 days** (conditional, low evidence) vs. 14 days

**Duration is counted from the first day of EFFECTIVE antibiotic therapy** — meaning an agent that achieves therapeutic levels in urine/tissue and is active against the causative pathogen. If initial empiric therapy was later found to be inactive (drug-bug mismatch), the clock restarts on the day the effective agent was started.

---

### IV-to-Oral Switch Decision Framework (Figure 1.3)

```
Patient receiving IV antibiotics for cUTI

    ┌────────────────────────┐
   YES                       NO
    │                         │
Assess oral options    Assess for drug-bug mismatch
    │                         │
Switch to oral agent   Assess for unresolved source
    │                         │
Treat for 7 days total  Reassess cUTI diagnosis
                              │
                         Plan for longer course
```

---

### Population-Specific Nuances

| Population | Duration | Notes |
|------------|----------|-------|
| Standard cUTI / pyelonephritis, improving | 5–7d (FQ) or 7d (non-FQ) | Recommendation I |
| Bacteremic cUTI, improving | 7 days | Recommendation II; BALANCE trial (n=3,608) supports |
| Men with suspected acute bacterial prostatitis | Consider 10–14 days | Evidence lacking; prostatitis may require longer penetration time |
| Severe sepsis, immunocompromise, CKD, abscess, complete obstruction | Individualized | Excluded from most duration trials; guideline does not formally cover |
| Indwelling urinary catheter | Individualized | Mostly excluded from trials; CAUTI duration not well-defined by this recommendation |

---

### Key Duration Evidence Notes

- **FQ evidence base** is stronger (7 of 10 trials used FQs) but was collected during a period of lower FQ resistance; generalizability to current resistance environment is uncertain.
- **Non-FQ evidence** is limited (1 direct trial; higher doses of oral beta-lactams may be required).
- **Bacteremia + 7 days:** Supported by BALANCE trial (3,608 patients; 7 vs. 14 days across bacteremia sources). Subgroup with urinary source showed no inferiority. Post-hoc signal of possible higher rehospitalization in men at 7 days — confounded by more comorbidities; not sufficient to change recommendation, but warrants individualization.
- **SJN alignment:** SJN specifies 7 days for complicated UTI across all scenarios. This aligns with the 2025 IDSA recommendation for non-FQ agents and bacteremic cUTI.

## Key Teaching Points for Case Design

1. **Sepsis status drives empiric breadth, not host risk factors** — DM, BPH, and immunocompromise do not automatically require carbapenem-level coverage. A septic patient with no resistant organism history → ceftriaxone or pip/tazo. A non-septic ESBL patient → carbapenem.

2. **ESBL history eliminates ceftriaxone and pip/tazo** — Prior ESBL-producing organism in culture is the specific risk factor that forces escalation to carbapenem, regardless of severity. Cases can test whether candidates recognize this override.

3. **Nitrofurantoin/fosfomycin are wrong answers for cUTI** — These are bladder agents. Prescribing either for pyelonephritis, bacteremic UTI, or any cUTI is a clear anti-pattern. The 2025 guideline makes this explicit.

4. **FQ exposure in past 12 months = avoid FQ empirically** — If a case includes recent FQ use in the history, selecting levofloxacin or ciprofloxacin empirically is the trap. Use ceftriaxone or pip/tazo instead.

5. **Carbapenems are stewardship-restricted for non-septic cUTI** — Prescribing meropenem for a febrile UTI without sepsis and no ESBL history is over-treatment. Cases testing appropriate antibiotic selection should reward restraint.

6. **Pip/tazo microbiologic cure is lower** — In RCTs, pip/tazo showed ~60% microbiologic eradication vs. 74% for comparators, despite similar clinical cure. This nuance is relevant for bacteremic UTI cases where organism clearance matters.

7. **Oral beta-lactams are inferior for cUTI step-down** — Amox-clav and cephalexin have consistently shown inferior outcomes vs. FQ/TMP-SMX for cUTI step-down. Use with ID pharmacist consultation if alternatives unavailable. Third-gen oral cephalosporins (cefpodoxime, cefixime) have more limited but potentially comparable data.

8. **Antibiogram threshold differs by sepsis severity** — Septic shock requires >90% susceptibility to use an agent empirically; sepsis without shock requires >80%. Non-septic patients don't trigger this requirement. This four-step framework is testable.

9. **National vs. SJN local divergence** — SJN escalates to cefepime or pip/tazo for septic shock/critically ill complicated UTI, and meropenem/ertapenem for known ESBL history. This aligns with the 2025 IDSA framework. SJN does not explicitly use the four-step antibiogram approach but the escalation logic maps cleanly.

10. **7 days is the standard duration for cUTI** — 10–14 days is no longer appropriate for cUTI when the patient is clinically improving. A case prescribing 14 days for standard pyelonephritis is over-treating per 2025 IDSA and SJN.

11. **Duration clock starts on day of effective therapy** — If empiric therapy was later proven to be inactive and changed, the 7-day clock restarts. This is testable: a case where empiric FQ was used but the organism was FQ-resistant, then switched to ceftriaxone on day 3 — total appropriate duration is 7 days from day 3, not day 1.

12. **Men with febrile UTI: rule out prostatitis before committing to 7 days** — If prostatitis is suspected, 10–14 days is appropriate. The 2025 IDSA explicitly carves this out as the primary exception to the 7-day recommendation.

13. **IV-to-oral switch is appropriate for bacteremic cUTI** — A case showing gram-negative bacteremia from a urinary source where the patient is improving should prompt oral step-down, not continuation of IV therapy. The guideline explicitly addresses bacteremia. Continuing IV for the full course is over-treatment in an improving patient with an effective oral option.

14. **Source control must precede oral switch in bacteremia** — If urinary obstruction is not resolved, switching to oral is premature. Cases can embed an unresolved obstruction as a contraindication to switch.

15. **Nitrofurantoin/oral fosfomycin are wrong answers for cUTI step-down** — Same reason they are wrong for empiric cUTI: inadequate tissue levels. This is reinforced in CQ2 as explicit exclusions. A case offering nitrofurantoin as an IV-to-oral option for pyelonephritis step-down is testing this anti-pattern.

16. **Cephalexin and amox-clav are inferior step-down choices** — Multiple studies show higher recurrence and readmission rates vs. FQ or TMP-SMX when these agents are used at standard doses. They are acceptable only when alternatives are unavailable, with ID pharmacist involvement. Cases presenting these as equivalent options are testing whether candidates recognize the hierarchy.

17. **Cefdinir is not appropriate for cUTI step-down** — 25% oral absorption and 13–23% urinary excretion. Retrospective data show higher UTI readmissions vs. FQ/TMP-SMX. Despite being a 3rd-gen cephalosporin, its pharmacokinetics disqualify it for this indication.

18. **GI dysfunction is a contraindication to IV-to-oral switch** — Ileus, severe vomiting, malabsorption, or active shock with gut hypoperfusion prevents reliable oral drug absorption. Cases can present these barriers as reasons to continue IV despite clinical improvement otherwise.
