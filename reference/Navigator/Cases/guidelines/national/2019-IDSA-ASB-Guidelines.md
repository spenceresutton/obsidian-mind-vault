# 2019 IDSA ASB Guideline

## Definition

**Asymptomatic bacteriuria (ASB):** ≥10⁵ CFU/mL in a voided urine specimen **without** signs or symptoms attributable to UTI.

- **Women:** 2 consecutive specimens required to confirm persistence. Between 10–60% of women do NOT have persistent bacteriuria on repeat screening after an initial positive — which is precisely why treating the first positive in an asymptomatic woman is unjustified.
- **Men:** Single specimen meeting quantitative criteria is sufficient for diagnosis.
- **Indwelling catheters:** ≥10⁵ CFU/mL remains the diagnostic threshold for bladder bacteriuria. Lower counts (10²–<10⁵ CFU/mL) from "in and out" catheterization or new catheter insertion may represent true bacteriuria but clinical significance is uncertain.

**Pyuria does NOT transform ASB into symptomatic UTI.** Pyuria is common in many populations with bacteriuria (older adults, catheterized patients, SCI) and does not indicate the need for treatment in the absence of localizing symptoms.

## Treat ASB In (the complete list)

| Population | Recommendation | Strength/Evidence |
|------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Pregnant women | Screen and treat | Strong / Moderate |
| Prior to invasive endourologic procedure with mucosal trauma | Screen and treat | Strong / Moderate |

**That is the complete list.** Every other population below is a nontreatment recommendation.

## Population-Specific Details

### Pregnant Women

- **Screen:** Single urine culture at one of the initial prenatal visits (early pregnancy).
- **Treat:** 4–7 days is preferred over shorter courses (weak recommendation, low evidence). Single-dose regimens are less effective for ASB in pregnancy.
- **Agents:** Standard UTI agents appropriate in pregnancy. Nitrofurantoin acceptable outside of 36–42 weeks (risk of neonatal hemolytic anemia at term). TMP/SMX less preferred in first trimester (folate antagonism) and near term (kernicterus risk). Fosfomycin has limited safety data in pregnancy — use with caution.
- **Evidence basis:** Untreated ASB in pregnancy → pyelonephritis risk (historical trials, moderate quality). This is the one population where bacteriuria has documented clinical consequences in the absence of symptoms.

### Older Adults — Delirium and Falls

Two specific scenarios the guideline addresses:

1. **Bacteriuria + delirium (acute mental status change, confusion):** If no local genitourinary symptoms and no systemic signs (fever, hemodynamic instability) → assess for other causes and observe. Do NOT attribute delirium to bacteriuria and treat.

2. **Bacteriuria + fall:** If no genitourinary symptoms and no systemic signs → assess for other causes. Do NOT treat bacteriuria as the cause of a fall.

**Values statement in the guideline:** Places high value on avoiding adverse effects of antimicrobials (C. diff, resistance, drug effects) in a vulnerable population where treatment benefit has not been demonstrated.

### Diabetes

- Against screening or treating. DM does NOT increase risk of progression from ASB to symptomatic UTI or to serious outcomes in a way that justifies treatment.
- Note: Most DM studies enrolled women; recommendation for men is inferred.

### Renal Transplant

- **>1 month post-transplant:** Strong recommendation against screening/treating (high-quality evidence — unusual for this guideline).
- **<1 month post-transplant:** Insufficient evidence; no recommendation made. Individualize.

### Solid Organ Transplant (Non-renal)

- Against screening/treating. Symptomatic UTI is uncommon post-SOT and risk of progression from ASB is negligible. Antimicrobial avoidance prioritized to limit C. diff and resistance.

### Spinal Cord Injury

- Against screening/treating. SCI patients have high bacteriuria prevalence and symptoms of UTI differ from classic genitourinary presentation (no normal sensation). Atypical symptom presentation creates uncertainty, but this does not justify routine treatment of bacteriuria. Symptomatic UTI criteria for SCI are defined in the International SCI UTI Data Set.

### Indwelling Urethral Catheter

- **Short-term (<30 days):** Do not screen or treat. Bacteriuria is nearly universal with prolonged catheter use and treatment does not reduce UTI risk — it promotes resistance.
- **Long-term:** Do not screen or treat. Same rationale.
- **At time of catheter removal:** No recommendation (knowledge gap). Antimicrobial prophylaxis at removal may confer some benefit but evidence is insufficient to formalize.
- **Suprapubic catheters:** Extrapolation of urethral catheter guidance is reasonable for both short-term and long-term suprapubic catheterization.

### Endourologic Procedures

**Invasive endoscopic procedure with mucosal trauma → screen and treat** (strong recommendation, moderate evidence):

1. Obtain urine culture prior to the procedure.
2. Prescribe **targeted (culture-guided) therapy**, not empiric therapy (weak recommendation, very low evidence).
3. **Short course: 1–2 doses**, not prolonged antibiotic course (weak recommendation, low evidence).
4. Initiate antimicrobials **30–60 minutes before the procedure**.

**Elective non-urologic surgery:** Against screening or treating (strong recommendation, low evidence).

## Key Teaching Points for Case Design

1. **ASB treatment is a binary decision, not a judgment call** — The 2019 IDSA guideline is categorical: treat only pregnant women and patients before invasive endourologic procedures. Every other population is a strong nontreatment recommendation. Cases that present bacteriuria in any other setting are testing whether candidates treat when they should not.

2. **Positive urine culture in an asymptomatic patient ≠ UTI** — The case design trap is presenting a positive culture in a non-qualifying patient and seeing if the candidate prescribes antibiotics. Wrong answer. The correct answer is to recognize ASB and withhold treatment.

3. **Pyuria does not indicate treatment** — A case can present bacteriuria + pyuria in an older adult without symptoms. Pyuria in older, catheterized, or SCI populations is not diagnostic of symptomatic UTI. Candidates who treat based on pyuria alone are falling into the over-treatment trap.

4. **Delirium is not a UTI symptom** — A case presenting an older adult with bacteriuria and confusion (no fever, no dysuria, no flank pain) is explicitly testing whether the candidate avoids attributing delirium to bacteriuria. The correct answer is to evaluate other causes and observe.

5. **Falls are not a UTI symptom** — Same principle. Bacteriuria + fall is not UTI. This is a direct guideline teaching point, not a clinical inference.

6. **DM, immunocompromise, and urological abnormalities do NOT indicate treatment** — A case can present a diabetic patient with incidental bacteriuria on a routine culture. The correct answer is nontreatment. (This aligns with the 2025 IDSA cUTI guideline's reclassification of these host factors as non-determinative.)

7. **Renal transplant >1 month: do not treat** — High-quality evidence supports this. A case showing a transplant patient with a positive surveillance culture who is asymptomatic should NOT be treated. The IDSA evidence grade here is unusually strong (high quality).

8. **Pre-endourologic procedure: culture first, target second, short course third** — Three sub-teaching points. Empiric prophylaxis without culture is a weaker recommendation. A 7-day course pre-procedure is over-treatment per the 1–2 dose guidance.

9. **Pregnancy is the one exception where early detection matters** — The treatment duration for ASB in pregnancy (4–7 days) is longer than a single dose. Cases can test the distinction between appropriate duration (4–7 days) and the inadequate single-dose approach sometimes used for uncomplicated cystitis.

10. **Women with two consecutive positive cultures are confirmed ASB, not symptomatic UTI** — The definition requires two specimens for women. A single positive culture in an asymptomatic woman is not confirmed ASB; it needs a repeat. Cases testing diagnostic rigor can include a single positive in an asymptomatic woman and see if the candidate repeats the culture.

11. **SJN alignment** — SJN's ASB protocol mirrors 2019 IDSA: treat ASB only in pregnancy and pre-urologic procedures. The approved UTI cases (ASB elderly KPC, ASB confounding symptoms, ASB pregnancy) all follow this framework. SJN does not diverge from national guidance on ASB indications.

## SJN vs. National Comparison

| Scenario | IDSA 2019 | SJN |
|----------|-----------|-----|
| Pregnant women | Treat | Treat |
| Pre-endourologic procedure | Treat (culture-guided, 1–2 doses) | Treat (culture-guided) |
| Elderly with delirium | Do not treat | Do not treat |
| Elderly LTCF | Do not treat | Do not treat |
| DM with incidental bacteriuria | Do not treat | Do not treat |
| Renal transplant | Do not treat (>1 month) | Do not treat |
| Short-term catheter (CAUTI) | Do not treat | Catheter removal is the intervention; antibiotics only for systemic symptoms |
| Long-term catheter | Do not treat | Do not treat |

SJN CAUTI guidance is more operationalized: the primary intervention for CAUTI is catheter removal, not antibiotic initiation. Antibiotic treatment is triggered by systemic symptoms (fever, hemodynamic instability), not positive culture alone.
