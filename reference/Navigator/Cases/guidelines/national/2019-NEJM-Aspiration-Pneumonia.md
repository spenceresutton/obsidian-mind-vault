# Aspiration Pneumonia — Mandell & Niederman, NEJM 2019

## Overview

Aspiration pneumonia accounts for 5–15% of CAP cases. It is not a distinct entity but part of a continuum that includes community- and hospital-acquired pneumonias. Robust diagnostic criteria are lacking — patient populations in studies are heterogeneous.

**Key distinction:** Aspiration pneumonia = infection caused by specific microorganisms. Chemical pneumonitis = inflammatory reaction to irritative gastric contents (acid/bile). Treatment differs substantially.

---

## Changing Microbiology

Anaerobes are no longer the dominant pathogen. The shift away from anaerobes is well-documented:

| Acquisition Site | Predominant Organisms |
|-------------------|-----------------------|
| Community-acquired | *S. pneumoniae*, *S. aureus*, *H. influenzae*, Enterobacteriaceae |
| Hospital-acquired | Gram-negative bacilli including *P. aeruginosa* — anaerobes NOT found in most studies |
| Institutionalized elderly | Gram-negative enteric bacteria (49%), anaerobes (16%), *S. aureus* (12%) |

**Teaching point:** Routine anaerobic coverage is NOT needed unless the patient has poor dental health AND develops necrotizing pneumonia or lung abscess. Empiric anaerobic coverage for garden-variety aspiration pneumonia is an anti-pattern.

---

## Risk Factors for Macroaspiration

**Impaired swallowing:**
- Dysphagia (OR 11.9 for pneumonia in elderly; present in 92% of elderly pneumonia patients)
- Esophageal disease: cancer, stricture, motility disorders
- COPD
- Oropharyngeal cancer

**Neurologic / CNS:**
- Seizures
- Multiple sclerosis, parkinsonism, stroke, dementia
- Cardiac arrest (pneumonia within 3 days in 65% of arrest patients)
- Post-extubation (swallowing dysfunction in ≥20% of extubated patients)

**Medications / Substances:**
- Narcotics, general anesthetics, antidepressants causing somnolence
- Alcohol
- Antipsychotics: increase risk by 1.5× (cohort of 146,552 patients)
- Sedatives, antihistamines

**Enteral feeding:** especially with gastric dysmotility, poor cough, altered mental status

**Compounding risk:** Dysphagia alone OR 9.4 for aspiration pneumonia; dysphagia + cerebrovascular disease OR 12.9

---

## Clinical Features

- Usually acute onset within hours to a few days after a sentinel aspiration event
- Anaerobic aspiration may be subacute (less virulent bacteria)
- Aspiration pneumonia has higher mortality than standard CAP (29.4% vs. 11.6%)
- In patients with documented dysphagia: bronchopneumonia > lobar (68% vs. 15%), 92% had posterior infiltrates
- Elderly patients with aspiration: higher serum sodium, worse renal function

**Chemical pneumonitis:** Sudden dyspnea, hypoxemia, tachycardia, diffuse wheezes/crackles. ARDS develops in up to 16.5% of witnessed aspirations. Low-pH aspirates are usually sterile initially.

---

## Imaging

Infiltrates occur in gravity-dependent segments:

| Patient Position During Event | Expected Infiltrate Location |
|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Supine | Superior segments of lower lobes OR posterior segments of upper lobes |
| Upright | Basal segments of lower lobes |

CXR can be negative early: 28% of CT-confirmed pneumonia cases were negative on CXR.

---

## Diagnosis

Based on:
1. Witnessed macroaspiration (or strong clinical history)
2. Known risk factors
3. Gravity-dependent infiltrates on imaging

**Procalcitonin:** NOT useful for distinguishing bacterial aspiration pneumonia from chemical pneumonitis. PCT on days 1 and 3 did not differentiate culture-positive pneumonia from culture-negative pneumonitis.

---

## Treatment Algorithm

### Community-Acquired Aspiration Pneumonia

**Normal dental health:**
- Ampicillin-sulbactam, amoxicillin-clavulanate, fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin or moxifloxacin), carbapenem (ertapenem), or ceftriaxone
- Do NOT add anaerobic coverage routinely

**Poor dental health:**
- Ampicillin-sulbactam, amoxicillin-clavulanate, moxifloxacin, or ertapenem
- Add clindamycin ONLY if poor dental health + necrotizing pneumonia or lung abscess

### Hospital-Acquired Aspiration Pneumonia

**No MDR risk factors:**
- Same agents as community-acquired above

**MDR risk factors present** (prior broad-spectrum abx within 90 days, ≥5 days hospitalization):
- Piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, levofloxacin, meropenem, or imipenem
- PLUS aminoglycoside or colistin if MDR coverage needed
- Add vancomycin or linezolid if documented MRSA nasal/respiratory colonization

### Chemical Pneumonitis (NOT aspiration pneumonia)

- Mild-moderate illness with infiltrate: **withhold antibiotics**, reassess at 48h
- Severe illness (intubated or septic shock): start empiric antibiotics regardless; reassess at 48–72h
- No role for glucocorticoids in either aspiration pneumonia or chemical pneumonitis

## Duration

- Good clinical response, no extrapulmonary infection: **5–7 days**
- Necrotizing pneumonia, lung abscess, empyema: longer; drainage may be needed
- No evidence to support glucocorticoids in routine management

---

## Key Teaching Points for Case Design

1. **Anaerobic coverage is a trap** — correct answer for aspiration pneumonia with normal dental health does NOT include metronidazole, clindamycin, or pip-tazo specifically for anaerobic coverage
2. **Aspiration ≠ anaerobes** — this was 1970s thinking; modern microbiology is dominated by aerobes
3. **Chemical pneumonitis vs. aspiration pneumonia** — the distinction determines whether antibiotics are needed at all; withhold antibiotics in mild-moderate pneumonitis
4. **CXR location is diagnostic** — posterior/gravity-dependent infiltrates are the signature; bilateral infiltrates suggest pneumonitis or aspiration of large volume
5. **Pseudomonas risk in HAP aspiration** — when hospital-acquired with MDR risk factors, P. aeruginosa coverage is appropriate; pip-tazo or cefepime ± aminoglycoside
6. **PCT cannot distinguish pneumonia from pneumonitis** — PCT-based escalation in aspiration syndromes is not evidence-based
7. **Dental health determines clindamycin** — poor dentition + necrotizing pneumonia/abscess is the only evidence-based indication for anaerobic coverage in aspiration
8. **Aspiration with Pseudomonas colonization** (CAP-09, CAP-10) — colonization provides a documented reservoir; the relevant CAP cases test whether you apply the appropriate anti-pseudomonal regimen in the right context, not whether aspiration itself requires anaerobic therapy
