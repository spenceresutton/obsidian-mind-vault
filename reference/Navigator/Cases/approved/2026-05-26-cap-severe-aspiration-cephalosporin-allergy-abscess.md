# CAP Case 13: Severe Aspiration Pneumonia with Cephalosporin Anaphylaxis and Cavitary Abscess

**Difficulty:** hard
**Author:** Spencer Sutton

## Prompt

SS is a 72-year-old male with a past medical history significant for ischemic stroke (3 months ago with residual dysphagia), hypertension, and atrial fibrillation who is brought to the emergency department by his wife with a 5-day history of productive cough, worsening fever, and declining functional status. He was hospitalized 8 weeks ago for his stroke and received IV antibiotics at that time for a suspected aspiration event during rehabilitation. He has had progressive difficulty swallowing since his stroke and has been on a pureed diet with thickened liquids. His wife reports multiple coughing episodes during meals.

In the ED, he received 2L of IV crystalloid for hypotension without adequate hemodynamic response. His MAP remained below 60 mmHg and norepinephrine was initiated. He was placed on 4L nasal cannula with persistent hypoxia and transferred directly to the ICU for further management.

On ICU arrival, vitals are: T 39.1°C, HR 116, BP 88/54 (MAP 65 on norepinephrine 0.10 mcg/kg/min), RR 28, SpO2 91% on 4L NC. He appears acutely ill with moderate respiratory distress. Lung exam reveals decreased breath sounds at the right base with dullness to percussion and coarse crackles over the right lower lobe. Oral secretions are visible and his gag reflex is weak. He is alert and oriented to person, place, and time. Speech is mildly dysarthric at baseline consistent with his prior stroke. He is cooperative but fatigued.

He takes apixaban 5 mg BID, metoprolol succinate 50 mg daily, lisinopril 10 mg daily, and atorvastatin 40 mg nightly. His allergy history is reviewed prior to antibiotic selection.

A chest X-ray is obtained on arrival followed by CT chest with contrast given severity of presentation and concern for complicated pneumonia. Labs are drawn and cultures are sent.

Ht: 5'11"   Wt: 82 kg   BMI: 25.5   Baseline SCr: 1.0 mg/dL   Allergies: Cephalexin (anaphylaxis)

## Expected Output

- **Presentation:** Severe aspiration community-acquired pneumonia with cavitary lung abscess in a patient with cephalosporin anaphylaxis.

- **Antibiotic Initiation** *(if applicable):*
  - Vancomycin — weight-based dosing per pharmacy protocol, target AUC/MIC 400–600
  - Aztreonam 2g IV q8h
  - Metronidazole 500mg IV q8h
  - Comment that atypical coverage is not indicated for aspiration pneumonia
  - Comment that anaerobic coverage is indicated for aspiration pneumonia given the cavitary lesion on CT
  - Comment that antipseudomonal coverage is indicated given IV antibiotics and hospitalization within the prior 90 days
  - Comment that MRSA coverage is not independently indicated given negative MRSA nasal PCR; however, vancomycin should not be discontinued — it provides streptococcal coverage that aztreonam lacks; a negative MRSA PCR does not eliminate the gram-positive gap when aztreonam is the only beta-lactam on board
  - Comment that treatment duration for lung abscess is typically 3–6 weeks, driven by clinical improvement and repeat imaging
  - **Do not recommend any beta-lactam therapies**

- **Treatment Optimization** *(if applicable):*
  - If cultures confirm anaerobic or mixed polymicrobial flora: continue vancomycin + aztreonam + metronidazole; no atypical coverage needed
  - If Streptococcus pneumoniae identified: continue vancomycin; de-escalate aztreonam and metronidazole if no gram-negatives or anaerobes identified; consult allergy for penicillin skin testing to explore introduction of a narrower oral agent for step-down
  - If Pseudomonas confirmed: continue aztreonam; reassess susceptibilities and adjust duration accordingly
  - Duration: minimum 3–4 weeks for lung abscess; extend to 4–6 weeks if abscess fails to resolve radiographically; obtain repeat CT at 4–6 weeks before stopping antibiotics
  - Abscess management: 2.6 cm is within the range managed with antibiotics alone; drainage (IR-guided or surgical) indicated if abscess enlarges, patient fails to improve after 1–2 weeks, or abscess exceeds approximately 6 cm; hold apixaban if drainage becomes necessary
  - Source control: speech therapy evaluation for post-stroke dysphagia; NPO and enteral nutrition via NG tube while acutely ill; long-term dysphagia management plan required given progressive aspiration risk from stroke
